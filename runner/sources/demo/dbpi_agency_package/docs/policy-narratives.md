# DBPI Policy Narratives

This document provides realistic policy narratives for the City of Red Vale Department of Building Permits and Inspections (DBPI). These narratives are intended to align the ontology with the agency's operating logic, decision points, and recordkeeping expectations.

Each narrative is written in business terms and is compatible with the concepts modeled in the ontology package.

---

## 1. Intake Completeness Policy

### Purpose
To ensure that the Department only advances applications that contain enough information to identify the property, the parties, the requested work, and the materials needed for review.

### Narrative
When an application is received, DBPI must determine whether it is sufficiently complete to create a formal reviewable case. A complete submission must identify the applicant, the affected property, the requested public service, the proposed work, and the supporting documents required by the service type. If essential information is missing, the application must not enter substantive review. Instead, the applicant receives a deficiency notice and the matter remains in an incomplete or pending state until cured.

### Operational implications
- A permit application is linked to a property, applicant, service, and supporting documents.
- The application carries an application status reflecting incomplete, submitted, in review, approved, denied, or withdrawn states.
- Completeness is distinct from approval; an application may be complete enough to review but still fail substantive code review.

### Typical records involved
- Permit Application
- Property / Parcel / Address
- Public Service
- Person or Organization
- Supporting Document
- Application Status

## 2. Jurisdiction and Authority Policy

### Purpose
To ensure that DBPI only acts on matters within its lawful jurisdiction and routes external matters appropriately.

### Narrative
Before substantive action is taken, the Department must confirm that the property or activity falls within the agency's jurisdiction and that the requested matter is of a type DBPI is authorized to regulate. If the matter is outside jurisdiction, the agency must document that fact, avoid acting beyond authority, and, where appropriate, direct the party to the proper department or external authority.

### Operational implications
- Properties are linked to jurisdictions.
- Cases outside jurisdiction should be traceable as closed, redirected, or otherwise non-actionable.
- Jurisdictional determinations are not informal; they must be recorded when they materially affect case handling.

### Typical records involved
- Property
- Jurisdiction
- Permit Application
- Violation Case
- Notes / external references

## 3. Plan Review Consistency Policy

### Purpose
To ensure that technical reviews are consistent, traceable, and tied to the governing code requirements.

### Narrative
All substantive plan review decisions must be tied to the controlling code sections, agency requirements, or adopted administrative rules that justify them. Reviewers may approve, conditionally approve, or require correction, but they may not rely solely on undocumented practice or personal preference. Comparable facts should produce comparable comments and outcomes. Where interpretation is uncertain, the issue should be elevated through supervisory review or a formal interpretation process.

### Operational implications
- Plan Review records are linked to Permit Applications.
- Review Comments are linked to the reviewer and to cited Code Sections.
- Approval history should remain traceable across multiple correction cycles.

### Typical records involved
- Permit Application
- Plan Review
- Review Comment
- Plan Reviewer
- Code Section
- Requirement

## 4. Permit Issuance Control Policy

### Purpose
To ensure that no permit is issued before required reviews, fees, and preconditions are satisfied.

### Narrative
A permit may be issued only when all required technical reviews have reached an approvable state, mandatory fees have been assessed and paid or otherwise lawfully deferred, and required pre-issuance conditions have been satisfied. Issuance authorizes only the work described in the permit and approved plans. Changes in scope require formal revision or supplemental approval rather than informal field acceptance.

### Operational implications
- An issued Permit is based on a Permit Application.
- Permit issuance has an issued date, expiration date, permit status, and authorized permit/work types.
- A permit should remain linked to the approved application, fees, requirements, and supporting documents.

### Typical records involved
- Permit Application
- Permit
- Fee
- Payment
- Requirement
- Supporting Document
- Permit Status

## 5. Inspection Evidence Policy

### Purpose
To ensure that field inspection outcomes are supported by documented observations and can be defended if challenged.

### Narrative
Inspection outcomes must be based on what the inspector observed at the site, within the scope of the permit or complaint, and against the applicable code or condition. The Department must preserve enough evidence to explain why the inspection passed, partially passed, failed, or was cancelled. When corrective items are identified, the result must be recorded in a way that clearly communicates what must be corrected before progress can continue.

### Operational implications
- Inspections are linked to the permit, property, assigned inspector, requested inspection type, result, and status.
- Material findings should cite code sections when appropriate.
- Inspection evidence may include notes, photos, measurements, or linked documents.

### Typical records involved
- Inspection Request
- Inspection
- Permit
- Property
- Inspector
- Inspection Type
- Inspection Result
- Code Section
- Supporting Document

## 6. Unsafe Condition Escalation Policy

### Purpose
To allow rapid escalation when the Department encounters imminent threats to life, health, or property.

### Narrative
When an inspector or investigator observes a condition that presents immediate danger, DBPI may escalate without exhausting the ordinary correction sequence. The Department may issue a stop-work order, emergency notice, unsafe-to-occupy determination, or other authorized emergency action. Emergency actions must still be documented, justified, and reviewable after the fact, but the Department is not required to delay action when delay would increase risk.

### Operational implications
- Serious inspection findings may be linked to enforcement matters.
- Emergency actions should be recorded distinctly from routine corrections.
- The escalation path should preserve the relationship between the triggering inspection, property, permit, and resulting case.

### Typical records involved
- Inspection
- Violation Case
- Notice of Violation or emergency notice
- Corrective Action
- Property
- Permit

## 7. Progressive Enforcement Policy

### Purpose
To promote voluntary compliance while preserving the ability to escalate against repeated or serious noncompliance.

### Narrative
DBPI generally seeks compliance through graduated enforcement. Minor or first-time issues may begin with correction notices or warnings. If the condition persists, recurs, or presents greater risk, the Department may escalate to formal violation notices, administrative penalties, hearings, stop-work orders, or legal referral. The Department's objective is lawful correction, not punishment for its own sake. However, repeated disregard of requirements, fraud, or danger to the public justifies more immediate formal action.

### Operational implications
- A violation case may contain notices, corrective actions, deadlines, appeal rights, and status changes over time.
- Enforcement history matters; recurrence influences proportionality.
- Cases should preserve the sequence of escalation.

### Typical records involved
- Violation Case
- Notice of Violation
- Corrective Action
- Appeal
- Hearing
- Case Status
- Property / Permit / Inspection links

## 8. Due Process and Appeal Policy

### Purpose
To ensure that regulated parties can challenge significant enforcement determinations through a documented process.

### Narrative
When DBPI issues an appealable enforcement decision, the affected party must have a clear avenue to contest it. Appeals must be linked to the underlying case, heard by a properly assigned hearing officer or other authorized decision-maker, and resolved through a documented outcome. The Department must maintain the evidentiary chain necessary to support its decision, including notices, inspection findings, cited code sections, and compliance history.

### Operational implications
- Appeals are linked to violation cases.
- Hearings are linked to appeals and hearing officers.
- Appeal status and hearing dates must be recorded.
- Evidence must remain traceable to the originating inspection, permit, property, or document.

### Typical records involved
- Appeal
- Hearing
- Hearing Officer
- Violation Case
- Notice of Violation
- Inspection
- Code Section

## 9. Records Traceability Policy

### Purpose
To ensure that every material agency action can be traced to the responsible actor, governing authority, and supporting record.

### Narrative
DBPI must maintain a defensible administrative record. Every material action—such as deeming an application incomplete, issuing a permit, failing an inspection, opening a violation case, issuing a notice, or deciding an appeal—must be traceable to the responsible employee or unit, the date of action, the affected matter, and the authoritative basis for the decision. This policy supports transparency, internal quality control, public records response, and legal defensibility.

### Operational implications
- Matters must be assigned to units and employees.
- Documents, code citations, statuses, and dates must be preserved.
- Cross-links among application, permit, inspection, enforcement, and appeal records are essential.

### Typical records involved
- Assigned unit / assigned employee
- Dates and identifiers
- Code Section
- Document
- Application, Permit, Inspection, Violation Case, Appeal

## 10. Timeliness and Queue Management Policy

### Purpose
To promote predictable service delivery without sacrificing legality or safety.

### Narrative
DBPI should process matters within published service targets whenever feasible. Managers are expected to monitor aging applications, overdue inspections, open correction cycles, and unresolved violation cases. Timeliness metrics are a management control, not a substitute for legal compliance: a matter may not be approved merely because a queue is old. However, the Department must use its operational data to identify bottlenecks, uneven workload distribution, or policy ambiguities that create avoidable delay.

### Operational implications
- Cases require dates sufficient for aging and queue analysis.
- Assignment and status data must support workload monitoring.
- Timeliness measures can be computed across applications, permits, inspections, and enforcement cases.

### Typical records involved
- Application status and submitted date
- Permit issued date and expiration date
- Inspection scheduled/completed dates
- Violation case opened/closed dates
- Assigned unit / assigned employee

## 11. Property-Centric Compliance History Policy

### Purpose
To allow the agency to understand the regulatory history of a site over time.

### Narrative
The Department's work is case-based, but many decisions require a property-centered view rather than a single-record view. Staff must be able to understand whether a property has prior permits, repeated failed inspections, recurring complaints, or repeated violations across multiple owners, tenants, or contractors. This historical perspective supports risk assessment, triage, enforcement proportionality, and policy analysis.

### Operational implications
- Applications, permits, inspections, and violation cases should all link back to the property.
- Properties may also link to parcels, addresses, owners, and jurisdictions.
- The model must support longitudinal history rather than isolated transactions.

### Typical records involved
- Property
- Parcel
- Address
- Owner
- Permit Application
- Permit
- Inspection
- Violation Case

## 12. Public Communication and Transparency Policy

### Purpose
To ensure that the Department communicates clearly with applicants, owners, contractors, and the public while preserving consistent application of rules.

### Narrative
DBPI must provide understandable explanations of status, requirements, and reasons for decisions. The Department may tailor communication to the audience, but it may not change the underlying rules from person to person. Public-facing communication should therefore derive from the same authoritative records used internally: application status, cited code sections, required corrections, issued notices, hearing dates, and related documents.

### Operational implications
- Status values and cited requirements must be fit for explanation.
- Supporting documents should be available to support communications and disclosure.
- Public counter and call-center staff benefit from the same traceable internal record as technical staff.

### Typical records involved
- Status concepts
- Code Section
- Review Comment
- Notice of Violation
- Hearing
- Supporting Document

## How these narratives support the ontology

These policy narratives are useful because they define why the ontology needs its major classes, properties, and status concepts. In particular, they justify:

- **organizational entities and assignment links** so responsibility is explicit;
- **property and jurisdiction entities** so matters are anchored to place and authority;
- **application, permit, review, and inspection entities** so the regulatory workflow is represented end to end;
- **violation, notice, corrective action, appeal, and hearing entities** so enforcement and due process are modeled; and
- **document, code section, status, date, and identifier fields** so traceability and explanation are preserved.
